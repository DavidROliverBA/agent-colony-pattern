# Beekeeping

## Structure
- A hive is a stack of boxes: brood chamber at the bottom, supers above.
- Each box holds removable frames of wax comb.
- Comb cells are used for brood, pollen, or honey.

## Members
- **Queen** — the only reproductive female; lays up to 1,500 eggs per day. Replaced by the colony when her pheromones fade; she does not rule.
- **Workers** — sterile females, the bulk of the colony. Roles rotate with age: cleaning, nursing, building, guarding, foraging.
- **Drones** — males whose only role is mating. Evicted by workers when autumn reduces available resources.

## Decision-making
- Swarming: when a colony outgrows its hive, a portion leaves with the old queen and scout bees use the waggle dance to converge on a new nest site. No central decision is made.
- Queen supersedure: when a queen weakens, workers quietly raise a successor and stop treating the old queen as queen.
- Colony decisions emerge from local signals, not from a controller.

## Beekeeper's role
- Inspect regularly (every week or two in season).
- Look for healthy brood pattern, adequate honey stores, signs of disease.
- Intervene to add storage, treat parasites, or replace a failing queen.
- Do not try to direct day-to-day behaviour.

## Mapping to software colonies
- Each agent = one bee: narrow, specialised, replaceable.
- Colony > any single agent.
- Decisions emerge from local signals + pre-registered review regimes.
- Human = beekeeper: notice, inspect, intervene only when the colony cannot correct itself.
